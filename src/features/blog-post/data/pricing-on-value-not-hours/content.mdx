## The Hourly Trap

When you price by the hour, every gain in skill works against you. The faster you shoot, the less you earn for the same result. You have spent years learning to nail a twilight exterior in twenty minutes, and the reward is a smaller invoice.

Clients do not buy your time. They buy a listing that sells faster and an agent who looks like the obvious choice. The hour is just the unit you happened to measure. Change the unit, and the whole conversation changes with it.

## Price the Outcome

Start from the result. A standard listing package is not "ninety minutes on site plus editing." It is "everything an agent needs to launch a property the day it hits the market." Photos, a walkthrough video, a floor plan, and a set of social cuts, delivered in one tidy handoff.

Framed that way, the price reflects what the deliverable is worth to a listing, not how quickly you can produce it. This is the heart of [value-based pricing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value-based_pricing). Two studios can shoot the same house in the same time and charge twice apart, because one sells minutes and the other sells momentum.

## Build Three Tiers

Most clients want to feel like they chose, not like they were sold. Three tiers give them that choice while quietly steering them toward the middle, which is where you want most of your volume to land.

- Essential: the photo set that gets a listing live with no fuss.
- Signature: photos, video, and a floor plan, built for the agent who markets every listing properly.
- Premier: the full launch kit with twilight, drone, and social cuts for flagship properties.

Name the tiers for the outcome, never for the file count. "Signature" sells; "Package B (24 photos)" does not.

## Anchor With Your Premium Package

List the premium tier first. It sets the reference point everything else is judged against through plain [anchoring](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anchoring_(cognitive_bias)>), and it makes the middle option feel like the sensible, grounded choice rather than the expensive one.

<Quote cite='Devin Cole'>
  The premium package does not have to sell often. Its job is to make the package you actually want
  to sell look reasonable.
</Quote>

## Handle the Pushback

Someone will always say another studio is cheaper. That is fine. You are not the cheapest, and pretending otherwise only trains clients to shop on price.

### Answer With the Outcome

Return to the result: a listing that photographs well sits on the market for fewer days, and fewer days is worth far more than the gap in your quote. Keep the math about their sale, not your invoice.

### Let the Wrong Clients Go

The agents who only hear the number were never going to be good clients. The ones who hear the value are the ones you want to keep for the next decade. Losing a price shopper is not a loss; it is room for a better fit.

## Review Your Numbers Quarterly

Pricing is not a decision you make once. Every quarter, look at which tier sells, how much editing each one really costs you, and where you are leaving money on the table. If the top tier never sells, it is priced wrong or described wrong. If the bottom tier sells most, your anchor is too low.

Treat the price list as a living document. The studios that thrive are not the ones that found the perfect number; they are the ones that keep adjusting it on purpose.
